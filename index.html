Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the questions we hear most from homeowners planning a project in Sonoma County.

Visit CSLB.ca.gov (California Contractors State License Board) and look up the contractor's license number. Confirm the license is active, the classification is Class B (General Building), and the bond and insurance are current. Always ask for the license number before signing any contract — a legitimate contractor will provide it immediately.
Yes — for any significant project, we recommend 2–3 detailed bids. Don't automatically choose the lowest bid. A low number often means inferior materials, unlicensed subs, or gaps in scope that show up as change orders once the project starts. Compare bids line by line. Ask each contractor what's not included. The best bid is detailed, realistic, and comes from a contractor with verifiable references and a clean CSLB record.
Ask: (1) What is your CA license number and can I verify it? (2) Who will be on-site managing my project daily? (3) How do you handle unexpected issues or change orders? (4) What does your payment schedule look like? (5) Can you provide 3 references from similar projects in the last 2 years? (6) Are all your subcontractors licensed and insured? A contractor who hesitates on any of these questions is a red flag.
In Sonoma County, a mid-range kitchen remodel (new cabinets, countertops, appliances, flooring) typically runs $40,000–$90,000. High-end custom kitchens with structural changes can reach $120,000–$200,000+. Key cost drivers: whether you're moving plumbing or electrical, cabinet quality (stock vs. semi-custom vs. full custom), countertop material, and overall square footage. We provide line-item estimates so you can see exactly where every dollar goes.
In Sonoma County, home additions typically cost $300–$550 per square foot for standard construction, and $600–$900+ for high-end finishes or complex structural work. Factors include foundation type, whether it's single or multi-story, plumbing included, roofline complexity, and finish level. A 600 sq ft ADU might run $180,000–$350,000 all-in depending on the site and utility requirements.
The industry standard is 10–20% above your quoted contract price. Older homes (pre-1970s) are more likely to reveal hidden issues once walls are opened — dry rot, outdated wiring, asbestos, or plumbing that needs upgrading. We communicate all discoveries immediately and never proceed with additional work without your written authorization. A thorough pre-construction walkthrough with us helps minimize surprises.
A complete fiberglass pool installation in Sonoma County typically runs $65,000–$110,000 including excavation, shell, plumbing, electrical, decking, and equipment. Gunite pools run higher ($90,000–$150,000+) due to custom shaping and plastering. Fiberglass pools are lower maintenance long-term — no replastering, smoother surface, faster install. We are an authorized Empire Fiberglass Pools dealer and can walk you through all three pool types in detail.
Cosmetic work (replacing fixtures, tile, or vanity in the same location) typically does not require a permit. Structural or system changes do — including moving walls, relocating plumbing, adding an electrical circuit, or changing the layout. Unpermitted work in California can affect your homeowner's insurance, create problems at resale, and result in fines. We handle all permit filing and coordination on any permitted work.
A home addition in Santa Rosa requires a Building Permit (structural), plus separate permits for Electrical, Plumbing, and Mechanical (HVAC). For larger additions, Planning Department review may also be required for zoning compliance (setbacks, floor area ratio). We have established relationships with the City of Santa Rosa and Sonoma County permit offices and handle the entire submission and follow-up process on your behalf.
Unpermitted work must be disclosed to buyers in California and can significantly complicate a sale. Buyers may demand the work be brought up to code (at your expense), lenders may refuse to finance the property, and insurance claims related to unpermitted work can be denied. The safest approach is always to permit and inspect construction properly. If you have existing unpermitted work, we can help assess it and determine the best path forward.
A standard kitchen remodel (no structural changes) takes 4–8 weeks from demo to final punch list. Complex remodels with structural modifications or custom cabinetry can extend to 10–14 weeks. The biggest variable is cabinet lead time — semi-custom cabinets: 3–6 weeks; full custom: 8–14 weeks. We pre-order all long-lead materials before demo begins so your kitchen isn't left open waiting on parts.

A straightforward room addition (500–800 sq ft, no major structural complexity) typically takes 3–5 months from permit approval to completion. Permit processing in Sonoma County adds 4–10 weeks before construction even begins. More complex additions with custom finishes, complex rooflines, or plumbing-heavy work can run 5–8 months. We provide a detailed schedule at the estimate stage and update it regularly throughout the project.

California law limits the initial deposit to 10% of the contract price or $1,000, whichever is less for home improvement contracts. After that, payments are tied to project milestones (framing complete, rough-in complete, drywall, etc.) — never paid ahead of work completed. We document each milestone and review it with you before requesting payment. Final payment is only due after your written sign-off on the punch list.
All Howard Construction projects include a 1-year workmanship warranty covering defects in labor and installation. California law also provides a 10-year structural defect warranty (Right to Repair Act) on new construction and major structural work. Manufacturer warranties apply separately to materials, appliances, and fixtures. We provide all warranty documentation at project completion and remain accessible for any post-project follow-up.
Every subcontractor we use is licensed, bonded, and insured in California. We work with a trusted network of plumbers, electricians, tile setters, and other specialists — many of whom we've worked with for 10+ years. We verify their CSLB license status before every project and require proof of insurance naming Howard Construction as additionally insured. We do not use day-labor or unlicensed tradespeople on any project.
The final walkthrough is specifically designed to catch and address any items that don't meet expectations before we consider the project complete. We create a written punch list together, and all items are corrected before final payment is requested. If a concern arises after project completion, contact Phil directly — we stand behind our work and will address legitimate workmanship issues promptly under our warranty.
